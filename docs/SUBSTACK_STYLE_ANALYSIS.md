# Substack Style Analysis for ARCHAI

Working note for shaping the ARCHAI Substack article into something technical, credible, and very human.

## Writers to Study

### Baratunde Thurston — Life With Machines

Why study him:
- He frames AI as a human, civic, playful, and cultural question rather than a pure product category.
- The publication promise is clear: clarity, critique, curiosity, and comedy.
- The tone gives readers permission to be thoughtful without becoming solemn.

What to borrow:
- Start from lived experience, not system architecture.
- Let humour and tenderness appear naturally.
- Treat readers as people trying to live with machines, not as users, clients, or stakeholders.
- Make the moral frame feel warm rather than preachy.

What not to copy:
- Do not become too host-like or entertainment-led. ARCHAI still needs research weight.

ARCHAI translation:
- "This is not about making museum objects fake-alive. It is about meeting curiosity at the moment it appears."
- Use phrases like "standing there with your phone", "the archive opens", "the conversation travels home".

### Casey Newton — Platformer

Why study him:
- He is very good at structure: scene, tension, why now, what changed, what it means.
- He keeps a single reporter's point of view visible, which builds trust.
- He explains technology through its consequences for institutions and democracy.

What to borrow:
- Clear section architecture.
- Strong thesis early.
- A visible "I" who has a reason for writing.
- Plain-language transitions: "Here is the problem", "The latest build adds", "That matters because".

What not to copy:
- Do not make ARCHAI sound like tech industry analysis only. It is heritage, practice, and design research.

ARCHAI translation:
- Keep one central argument: ARCHAI makes cultural knowledge conversational while keeping authority with the institution.
- Use short explanatory sections and avoid academic throat-clearing.

### Brian Merchant — Blood in the Machine

Why study him:
- He writes about technology through power, labour, and lived harm.
- He foregrounds human testimony and moral stakes.
- He makes technical systems feel consequential without flattening them.

What to borrow:
- Name the power problem clearly: who owns the machine, the model, the data, and the conversation?
- Keep ordinary people in frame: visitors, curators, volunteers, regional museums, families.
- Admit what technology does not solve.

What not to copy:
- Do not make the piece too combative. ARCHAI needs invitation as much as critique.

ARCHAI translation:
- The villain is not "AI". The problem is outsourcing cultural memory to remote systems that institutions cannot inspect, repair, or govern.

### Alex Kantrowitz — Big Technology

Why study him:
- Strong at broad context and clean explanatory prose.
- Useful model for making technical developments feel current and consequential.

What to borrow:
- Crisp technology explanation.
- One idea per paragraph.
- Keep context tight.

What not to copy:
- Avoid generic Silicon Valley newsletter voice.

## Delivery Principles for This Article

1. Open with a human scene.
   Do not open with "ARCHAI is..." Open with Phar Lap, a phone, a visitor, and curiosity.

2. Let the build story be personal.
   Say plainly that this was built from scratch, on a Mac, with Claude and Codex as studio tools. Do not hide the process. It is part of the research.

3. Explain the tech only after the reader cares.
   The order should be: object -> question -> experience -> why existing systems fail -> architecture.

4. Keep sovereignty practical.
   Sovereignty is not a slogan. It means the institution owns the hardware, model, data, logs, and decisions.

5. Stay honest about AI.
   Avoid "no hallucination". Say "source-grounded", "bounded", "designed to reduce confabulation", "says when it does not know".

6. Keep the reader near people.
   Mention visitors, curators, families, teachers, researchers, volunteers, regional museums.

7. Use warm declarative lines.
   Example: "The conversation can travel, but the evidence boundary travels with it."

8. End with invitation, not triumph.
   The piece should feel like: I built a working alternative. Come and test it with me.

## Recommended Tone Formula

Baratunde warmth + Casey structure + Merchant stakes + Rob's maker voice + a little punk/open-source refusal.

In practice:
- human first
- clear argument
- moral spine
- concrete technical detail
- no hype
- no fake certainty
- enough weirdness to feel alive

## Punk / Counterculture Edge

Use this as seasoning, not the whole meal.

The ARCHAI edge:
- Anti-lock-in, not anti-institution.
- Anti-platform dependency, not anti-technology.
- Open-source and local-first as cultural self-defence.
- Small machines, real code, visible logs, human judgement.
- Build the alternative instead of only critiquing the problem.

Good lines:
- "The criticism is the thing I made."
- "Own the machine, own the model, own the conversation."
- "Institutions should not have to rent their own memory back from a platform."
- "One object. One page. One working pathway. Then another."

Avoid:
- Empty rebellion posture.
- Sneering at curators, museums, or cautious institutions.
- Making open source sound careless with cultural authority.
- Overclaiming sovereignty as purity. Keep it practical.
