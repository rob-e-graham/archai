# Strategy Note: Access As Product, And AI With Strings Attached

Date: 2026-07-11
Status: field-signal capture + positioning note
Source: two LinkedIn signals seen in-feed (screenshots archived), read against ARCHAI's current roadmap.

This note captures two related conversations moving through the museum field
right now, pulls out what is vital enough to steer the roadmap, and drafts a
public response. It is a working note, not a public post. The response draft at
the end is the thing meant for outside eyes.

---

## Signal 1 — "Museums are showing you what they used to hide"

Regina Connell (Relevance, Resonance), 1d, edited. Distilled:

- Museums are exposing the work they used to keep behind closed doors, and it is
  irresistible to audiences.
  - Rijksmuseum let the public watch the *Night Watch* restored **live, behind glass**.
  - The V&A opened a quarter-million objects to **open storage**.
  - The Smithsonian gave its conservation lab **floor-to-ceiling windows**.
- Claudio Buda named this first as a **curatorial shift**. Connell argues it is
  more than that.
- The deeper claim: *we no longer trust the finished, polished version of
  anything.* We trust the seams, the mess, the hand at work. We crave going
  behind the curtain.
  - Luxury already monetises this: Hermès has run a school, a traveling
    exhibition, and a documentary series around **watching craftspeople work**
    for ~20 years. Cartier does a private version for its best clients.
- The provocation: **museums have the same "access luxury" that luxury brands
  do — and have not turned it into something.**
  - "Watch the conservator work" is today a perk buried in a membership tier,
    "somewhere between the gala invite and free coat check."
  - It *could — should — be a product.* With a name. A price. A deeper way to
    engage with the museum.
  - "What would you pay to watch a 400-year-old painting get its varnish
    stripped, layer by layer? That's pretty juicy."

## Signal 2 — "AI gives museums a lifeline — with strings attached"

The Art Newspaper / FT-style feature by Naomi Rea (shared by Vikas Lohchab;
illustration by Pete Reynolds). Distilled:

- Facing dwindling visitor numbers and tight budgets, museums are adopting AI
  characters and chatbots to revive exhibitions and engagement.
  - Leighton House ran a voiced AI model of Frederic, Lord Leighton via QR codes.
  - The Met built a chatbot mimicking a 20th-century socialite; the Dalí Museum
    reanimated the painter by phone; an AI Theodore Roosevelt appeared at a
    Presidential Library.
  - Cultural strategist **András Szántó** frames conversational tech as the
    inevitable *"language of our time, our future."*
- The catch, per **Nataliya Kosmyna (MIT Media Lab)**: unreliable tools risk
  **"cultural homogenisation."** Generative AI has been shown to weaken memory
  pathways and problem-solving, and to homogenise thinking. To stop heritage
  being diluted into "AI art slop," cash-strapped institutions must manage their
  data **with absolute care.**
- Ask Mona's Marion Carré: bespoke models trained on an institution's own
  unvetted-but-owned material are "qualitatively different from popular LLMs
  trained on vast tracts of internet text… garbage in, garbage out."
- The Natural History Museum's Doug Gurr: museums sit on **a trove of specialist
  knowledge — data — that is a font of untapped revenue.** London's Imperial War
  Museum and the V&A are in a UK-government-backed pilot marketplace licensing
  high-quality data to AI developers. Gurr's caution: public institutions should
  stop giving database access to commercial users for free, and any partnership
  must be "hard-wired" so cultural and ethical considerations, and the
  institution's mission, survive the commercial deal.

## Why these two signals belong in the same note

They are the same argument from two directions:

- Connell says the museum's scarcest asset is **authentic access** — the seam,
  the hand, the process — and it is under-monetised.
- The AI feature says the museum's scarcest asset is **specialist, trustworthy
  data** — and it is being either given away for free or diluted into slop.

Both land on the same imperative: **the institution should own, name, and
govern the thing that makes it trustworthy — and not rent it back from a
platform, or leak it away for free.** That is ARCHAI's whole thesis.

## Where this maps onto ARCHAI

ARCHAI already argues for sovereign, source-grounded, rights-aware heritage
infrastructure. These signals sharpen three things we can say more sharply:

1. **"Behind the curtain" is a native ARCHAI surface, not a bolt-on.**
   Our object pages already carry provenance, rights/licence status, source
   URL, and interpretation as first-class, visible metadata. "Watch the process"
   is the same instinct: expose the working layer, not just the polished label.
   The conservation-camera version is theirs; the *documented-record* version is
   ours. We show the seams of the record: where it came from, what is verified,
   what the machine does and does not know.

2. **"Absolute care with data" is our default, not their afterthought.**
   Kosmyna's homogenisation warning and Carré's "garbage in, garbage out" are
   exactly why ARCHAI preserves raw records, normalises into a canonical layer,
   keeps rights/provenance/language/protocol visible, and treats speech,
   translation, and AI interpretation as **optional, documented, replaceable
   layers**. Our answer to "AI slop" is source-grounding and a visible evidence
   boundary — the model says when it does not know.

3. **The data-marketplace moment is a governance opportunity, not just a
   revenue one.** IWM/V&A licensing into a government-backed marketplace is the
   headline; Gurr's "hard-wired" ethics and mission clause is the real story.
   ARCHAI's position: sovereignty means the institution keeps the hardware,
   model, data, logs, and decisions — so it can enter (or refuse) a data deal
   from a position of control rather than dependency. Own the machine, own the
   model, own the conversation.

### What to be careful about (so we don't become the thing we critique)

- Do **not** pitch ARCHAI as "monetise access with AI." The access-as-product
  idea is Connell's, and it is a *human-access* argument (real conservators,
  real process). Our contribution is the trustworthy record underneath it, not
  a paywall bot.
- Do **not** overclaim "no hallucination." Keep our honest language:
  source-grounded, bounded, designed to reduce confabulation, says when it does
  not know.
- Keep curators in frame. The seam-and-hand story is a story about **human
  labour made visible.** ARCHAI should make that labour more legible, never
  replace or obscure it.

## Vital items promoted to the roadmap

Folded into `ROADMAP.md` as a new theme ("Field Signals → Product Language").
Summary of what was promoted:

- **Evidence-boundary as a visible product feature.** Make "what the system
  knows, what it doesn't, and where it got it" a named, demonstrable surface —
  the direct answer to the "AI slop / homogenisation" fear.
- **"Behind the record" framing for object detail.** Lean into the provenance /
  rights / source-link layer as the ARCHAI equivalent of open storage and the
  conservation window: the value is in showing the working, not hiding it.
- **Data-sovereignty talking point for partner/funder conversations.** Position
  ARCHAI against the free-give-away and rent-it-back extremes surfaced by the
  IWM/V&A marketplace story, for the RMIT/pre-seed and outreach decks.

These are *positioning and framing* items, not new engineering milestones — they
sharpen how we describe features we largely already have.

> conservator" is irresistible is the same reason the AI-in-museums story (Naomi

> the polish. Audiences want the process *and* the receipts.
>
> So if access becomes a product, the thing you're really selling isn't the
> spectacle of the varnish coming off — it's the museum's authority to say "this

> institutions are either giving it away free to AI developers or watching it get
> flattened into slop.
>
> The behind-the-curtain move and the data-governance move are the same move:
> own the record, show the working, don't rent your own memory back from a

> rights-visible, runs on the institution's own machine. Happy to show anyone
> who wants to poke at it.

### Longer version (standalone post)

> **Museums are showing the seams. The harder question is who owns them.**
>
> Two things crossed my feed this week. Regina Connell on museums turning
> "behind the curtain" — the *Night Watch* restored behind glass, V&A open
> storage, the Smithsonian's glass-walled lab — into something you could name
> and price, the way Hermès sells you the sight of a craftsperson at work. And
> Naomi Rea on museums reaching for AI to survive shrinking budgets, with MIT's
> Nataliya Kosmyna warning it could homogenise heritage into "AI slop" unless
> institutions manage their data with absolute care.
>
> They're the same story. Both are about the one thing a museum has that a
> platform doesn't: **authentic, accountable access to the real thing.** The
> conservator's hand. The provenance. The record of how we know what we claim.
>
> Connell is right that this is under-monetised. But the asset underneath the
> experience is *data* — specialist, hard-won, trustworthy. And right now most
> institutions are doing one of two risky things with it: giving it to AI
> developers for free, or letting a generic model blur it into something that
> sounds confident and knows nothing.
>
> There's a third option. Show the working *and* keep the keys. Put the
> provenance, the rights, the source, and the limits of what the machine knows
> on the surface where visitors can see them. Run it on hardware the institution
> owns. Let the model say "I don't know" out loud. Make the seam visible, and
> make it yours.
>
> That's what I've been building — an open, source-grounded, rights-aware
> heritage system that runs local-first, so a museum can open the curtain without
> renting its own memory back from a platform. The criticism is the thing I made.
> If you work in a collection and want to break it, come find me.
