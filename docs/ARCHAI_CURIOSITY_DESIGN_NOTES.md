# Curiosity as a design constraint — notes from Le Cunff

**Source:** Anne-Laure Le Cunff, "We Are Losing the Ability to Discover What We Didn't Know to Ask,"
*The New York Times* (Opinion), 8 July 2026. Print edition 12 July 2026 as "The Space Between a
Question and an Answer Has Value." Le Cunff is a neuroscientist at the Institute of Psychiatry,
Psychology and Neuroscience, King's College London.
**Status:** design thinking, feeding the essay, AUXIO prompt design, and the tour/wayfinding work.

---

## 1. Her argument, compressed

- Most AI tools compress the journey from question to answer into an instant arrival. Over 60% of
  US Google searches now end without a click. The explorative phase of search is disappearing.
- Neuroscience of curiosity: a *gap between what you want to know and what you find out* puts the
  brain into heightened receptivity. While a question is open, reward circuits activate, the
  hippocampus readies, and **unrelated information encountered in the waiting is remembered far
  better** ("incidental learning"). Replicated across infants, children, adults.
- **The window stays open only while the question is unanswered.** A three-second AI summary closes
  it before curiosity can deepen. The danger is not that people stop asking questions; it is that
  **questions become endpoints.**
- Her asks of AI builders: keep sources visible; show competing explanations rather than one smooth
  paragraph; offer modes that reward exploration over speed. "The space between a question and an
  answer has value, and that value should not be engineered away."

## 2. Why this matters to ARCHAI

ARCHAI already violates the answer-machine pattern in one load-bearing way: **an object that
declines rather than invents leaves the gap open.** Where a chatbot's fluent guess closes the
curiosity window with counterfeit completeness, "the record doesn't say" is — in Le Cunff's terms —
a window held open. Every gap in a record is an invitation; her work gives that line a
neuroscientific backbone rather than a poetic one.

She also supplies the scientific case for three things ARCHAI does by conviction:

| Le Cunff's ask | ARCHAI's existing answer |
|---|---|
| Keep sources visible | Every answer traceable to the record; visible evidence trail |
| Show competing explanations | Contribution pathway: community memory and correction entering beside the institutional record |
| Reward exploration over speed | Related objects, semantic adjacency, tours, wayfinding — the visit as territory, not query→answer |

And one warning she aims at us as much as anyone: a *grounded* answer is still an answer. If an
AUXIO object answers perfectly and stops, we have built a very honest endpoint. The design goal is
an object that answers **and re-opens the window.**

## 3. Design rules to adopt

1. **Answer + doorway.** An object's grounded answer should, where natural, end with an opening:
   what the record *doesn't* say, a question back to the visitor, or one adjacent object ("the work
   beside me came from the same workshop — ask it about the glaze"). Never a hard stop when a soft
   one exists. → AUXIO prompt design.
2. **The unknown is content.** Surface the gaps deliberately: "three things nobody has answered
   about me" is a stronger invitation than a complete-sounding summary. Pairs directly with the
   contribution pathway — the visitor holding the missing piece is the loop from the essay
   (Adeline). → AUXIO page fields + prompt.
3. **Adjacency is engineered serendipity.** Le Cunff's incidental learning is our related-objects
   rail and the physical+semantic fusion in wayfinding ("something thematically close sits two
   galleries over"). Treat those not as navigation conveniences but as the *learning mechanism* —
   which argues for keeping one unexpected/loose association in every related set, not only the
   safest matches. → converse results, tours, spatial doc.
4. **Slow modes are a feature.** The tour planner and "what's around me?" narration reward wandering
   over retrieval. Position them as such — a museum is one of the last places designed for
   undirected exploration, and ARCHAI should amplify that rather than efficiency-ing it away.
5. **Never one smooth paragraph where two voices exist.** Where a community contribution and an
   institutional record both speak, show both, attributed — do not compress them into a single
   authoritative answer.

## 4. Where to use it

- **Essay:** cite her in the *"honesty turns out to be the most interesting thing an object can
  do"* passage — the claim currently stands on experience; her research makes it evidence-backed.
  Also available as a receipt: the "questions become endpoints" line is the sharpest published
  statement of what AUXIO is built against.
- **Pitches/pilots:** "an object that admits what it doesn't know keeps visitors curious — and the
  neuroscience says curious visitors learn and remember more of *everything* around them." That is
  a museum-education outcome, which is the language education teams buy in.
- **Prompt work (near-term):** implement rule 1 (answer + doorway) in the AUXIO visitor prompt and
  rule 2 as an optional "open questions" block on AUXIO pages.
